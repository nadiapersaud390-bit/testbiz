⚠️ Criminal Alert
Money Laundering — Gov. Ties
▼

If a caller claims they have ties with the government, are moving funds "on behalf of" an agency, or the situation feels like they're trying to legitimize large suspicious transfers — this is a money laundering red flag. End the call and report it.

💡 What Is Money Laundering?

Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained money appear legitimate. Criminals run "dirty" money through businesses, loans, or financial systems to disguise its origin. Claiming government ties is a common tactic to make the transaction seem official and bypass scrutiny.

"I work with the government / federal agency"
"These funds are authorized / government-backed"
Moving unusually large sums through a new or vague business

This is a prank. End the call professionally and move on to the next one. If the number keeps coming in, pass it to your team leader.

End call professionally & move on.

🚨 Prank Caller Red Flags

Spot these signs early — end the call professionally and move on

1. Yes to Everything

Real owners push back, ask questions, or pause to think. If someone says "yes" to every question instantly — they're not real.

Watch for: robotic "yes yes yes," zero hesitation on every answer

2. Generic or Fake Business Name

Vague names like "My Company," "Business LLC," or names that change when you repeat them back are fake.

Watch for: "um… it's like… my business," name changes when repeated

3. Asking for Money / Reversing the Script

If they flip the call — "So how much will YOU give me?" before you've even qualified them.

Watch for: "just send it," "how fast can I get it," "give me the max," "I need it now," "I need it today," "I need it in 24 hours"

4. Laughing, Whispering, or Coached

Giggling, muffled laughter, or someone whispering answers in the background means group prank.

Watch for: echoing, whispering, covering the phone, coached replies

5. Inconsistent or Absurd Details

Revenue of $50 million but open only 2 months? Real owners are consistent because they live their business every day.

Watch for: contradictory info, impossible numbers, changing their story

6. Refuses to Stay On Topic

Prankers derail the call with jokes and random questions.

Watch for: tangents, jokes about the call, refusing to answer directly

7. Suspicious Tone & Energy

Real owners are slightly guarded, professional, measured. Prank callers sound too casual, overly excited, or flat and robotic.

Watch for: giggling, overly eager, robotic monotone, acting a role

8. "Justin Told Me" / Asks About Portal

If a caller says someone named "Justin" told them they have a loan and to log into a portal to make a payment or collect funds — this is a scam script, not a real customer.

Watch for: "I was told to log in," "where's the portal," "Justin said I'm approved"

9. Claims Government or Official Ties

Callers who claim government connections or say funds are "federally authorized" to pressure you into bypassing normal process are attempting fraud or money laundering.

Watch for: "federal agency," "government authorized," "official program," "ties to [agency]"

🎙️ Tone & Voice Tells

Real owner vs. prank caller — side by side

✅ Real Owner Sounds Like

  • • Slightly busy or distracted
  • • Asks "what is this for exactly?"
  • • Pauses to recall their revenue
  • • States business name confidently
  • • Skeptical but willing to listen
  • • Professional, measured tone
  • • Asks about process / next steps
  • • Background sounds like a real workplace

🚨 Prank Caller Sounds Like

  • • Too casual or overly excited
  • • Agrees to everything instantly
  • • Never hesitates on any answer
  • • Gives absurd or vague business name
  • • Giggles or sounds entertained
  • • Flat robotic tone, reading a script
  • • Keeps pushing for the money amount
  • • Background sounds like a group or party

End the call: "Thank you for your time, we're not able to proceed. Have a great day."
